Every Few Years, The Country Goes Off

I am Korean. I grew up inside these explosions.

Case One · 1998 — 2021

The PC bang

In 1998, there were about a hundred PC bangs in Korea. Basement rooms with a dozen computers each, open all night, smelling faintly of ramen. Broadband was rolling out. StarCraft had just released. Then something happened to the curve.

  1. 1998 just a few rooms

    A hundred basements. You’d walk past one on the way home from school.

  2. 2001 one on every corner

    Three years on, there were fifteen thousand. Korea had broadband before most of America did. There was now a PC bang on every corner in the way there are now cafes on every corner — it was not a figure of speech.

  3. 2009 critical mass

    At the peak: 21,549. One for every 2,400 Koreans. A country the size of Kentucky had opened twenty-five PC bangs a week for eleven years.

  4. 2015 the air has changed

    The iPhone arrived in 2009. Mobile games followed. The basements started closing, one by one, with nobody mourning them specifically.

  5. 2021 what remains

    By 2021, 9,265 remained. Still more than most countries ever had. A craze is a thing that happens to a country, and then the country is a different country, and the next craze is already starting somewhere else.

The Pattern

The Pattern

I am Korean. I have watched this happen to my own neighborhood four or five times.

The shape repeats. A Korean craze does not rise the way trends rise in other places. It ignites. Four years, five years, and the country has reorganized itself around a thing that did not previously exist. Then the craze either saturates and becomes infrastructure, or it burns out and leaves behind the shells of itself.

What gets called a boom in English is a weaker word than what actually happens. A boom implies enthusiasm. This is closer to what entomologists call gregarization — the point at which individual locusts, after reaching a density threshold, stop being individuals and start behaving as a single organism moving in one direction.

When enough of us notice something, we all notice it at once, and then we are all doing it, and then the doing is the only option.

Korean density, Korean group chats, Korean lifetime competition produce this.

Case Two · 2000 — 2024

The coffee shop

There are now more than a hundred thousand coffee shops in Korea. Seventeen thousand in Seoul alone. One for every seven hundred residents. Seoul has more Starbucks stores than any city in the world, including New York.

  1. 2000 just a few rooms

    A handful of chains. Mostly imported. A novelty.

  2. 2008 one on every corner

    By 2008, twelve thousand. The dabang — traditional tea rooms — had mostly closed. Nobody sent them off.

  3. 2016 one on every corner

    Fifty thousand. “Cafe” stopped being a kind of shop and became a kind of room.

  4. 2022 critical mass

    Between 2018 and 2022, the count doubled. Not grew — doubled. A country that already had more cafes than any reasonable country should have added as many again in four years.

  5. 2024 critical mass

    One hundred thousand. This one never declined. It just kept going until it was the texture of the city itself.

Case Three · 1998 — 2010

The jjimjilbang

The public bathhouse was old. The jjimjilbang was new. In the late 1990s, the old ones started disappearing and the new ones started opening — bath complexes the size of shopping malls, with saunas and sleeping rooms and restaurants and karaoke.

  1. 1998 just a few rooms

    A different kind of bath, introduced as a small upgrade to an old institution.

  2. 2003 something is happening

    January 19 became Jjimjilbang Day because 119 sounds like “fire” in Korean. This was marketing. It worked.

  3. 2010 critical mass

    By the mid-2000s, a jjimjilbang was where you went on a first date. This was covered in dramas. The dramas made it look normal, which it now was.

Case Four · 2020 — 2026

The run

Between 2020 and 2024, the number of marathons held in Korea went from 19 to 254.

  1. 2020 just a few rooms

    Nineteen marathons in the country. A normal year. A small community.

  2. 2022 something is happening

    A hundred and twenty. Something had happened to the curve.

  3. 2024 critical mass

    Two hundred and fifty-four marathons. More than a million participants. Spending at running specialty stores up 216% in two years. A single running brand reported a 74% sales increase.

  4. 2026 critical mass

    The thirty-somethings are leading this one. The generation that grew up in the PC bangs, studied in the cafes, dated at the jjimjilbang. They are not watching the boom. They have become the boom.

The Comparison

Korea vs. United States marathons

The United States runs about the same number of marathons it has always run. Korea, in four years, produced a thirteenfold increase.

Close

I am writing this in 2026. My street in Seoul has four running stores that did not exist two years ago. There is a cafe on one corner, a study cafe on the opposite corner, and a jjimjilbang three blocks down. The PC bang on the other side of the subway station closed last year. In its place is a pilates studio, which I have been told is the next one.

I cannot say whether this is exhausting or exhilarating. Those are both words for the same thing when you are inside it. What I can say is that the next craze is already forming somewhere, and in three years it will be impossible to miss on the walk to the subway, and in five years we will have moved on to whatever came after.

We do not catch trends. We complete them. What other countries spread across a generation, we finish in four years and forget in six. The running boom will end the way all of them end — not because it burned out, but because something else has already started, and we have already noticed, and we are already there.
